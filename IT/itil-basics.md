# itil-basics.md
**Using ITIL Concepts with AI in Real IT Operations**

## Purpose of this document

This file introduces core ITIL concepts in a **practical, operational way**, and explains how AI can support ITIL-aligned thinking without turning ITIL into bureaucracy.

This is not an ITIL exam guide.  
It is a working reference for applying ITIL principles in daily IT work.

## Core idea

**ITIL is about delivering value through services.  
AI is a tool that can support that goal, not a replacement for process ownership.**

Good ITIL implementation improves outcomes.  
Bad ITIL implementation produces paperwork.

AI should strengthen judgment, visibility, and consistency, not add noise.

## What ITIL is (and is not)

ITIL is:

- A framework for managing IT services
- Focused on value, outcomes, and reliability
- Adaptable to different organization sizes

ITIL is not:

- A rigid set of rules
- A guarantee of quality
- A substitute for technical competence

ITIL describes *what* to think about, not *how* to think.

## Core ITIL concepts (simplified)

### Service

A service delivers value by enabling outcomes customers want, without them managing underlying risks.

AI can help clarify:
- Who the service is for
- What outcomes matter
- What failure looks like

### Value

Value is co-created between provider and user.

AI can assist by:
- Analyzing usage patterns
- Identifying friction points
- Highlighting unmet expectations

### Risk

Risk is the uncertainty of outcomes.

AI can:
- Surface known risk categories
- Assist with scenario analysis
- Support risk-aware decisions

Risk ownership remains human.

## Key ITIL practices and how AI supports them

### Incident management

Goal: Restore service as quickly as possible.

AI can help:
- Triage incidents
- Suggest likely causes
- Recommend safe diagnostic steps

AI should not:
- Make unverified production changes

### Problem management

Goal: Prevent recurrence.

AI can help:
- Identify patterns across incidents
- Support root cause analysis
- Document known errors

AI works best after stabilization.

### Change enablement

Goal: Control risk while enabling change.

AI can help:
- Assess change impact
- Identify dependencies
- Evaluate rollback readiness

Final approval and accountability remain human.

### Service request management

Goal: Fulfill standard requests efficiently.

AI can help:
- Classify requests
- Route tickets
- Draft responses

Requests still need clear ownership.

### Continual improvement

Goal: Improve services over time.

AI can help:
- Analyze trends
- Suggest improvement opportunities
- Track metrics

Improvement requires intentional action, not just insight.

## How AI fits into ITIL thinking

AI aligns naturally with ITIL when used for:

- Pattern recognition
- Consistency enforcement
- Knowledge retrieval
- Decision support

AI conflicts with ITIL when used to:
- Bypass controls
- Justify poor decisions
- Replace accountability

## Practical ITIL-aligned AI prompts

### Incident triage

> Given this incident description, help classify severity and suggest initial investigation steps.

### Change impact assessment

> Analyze the potential impact of this change on services, dependencies, and users.

### Problem trend analysis

> Review these incidents and identify patterns that may indicate an underlying problem.

### Service improvement

> Based on service performance data, suggest areas where improvements would deliver the most value.

## Common ITIL misuses with AI

- Treating AI recommendations as approvals
- Automating broken processes
- Adding AI without defining service ownership
- Measuring activity instead of outcomes

AI amplifies whatever process it touches.  
If the process is weak, the result is faster failure.

## When not to apply ITIL strictly

Strict ITIL process may not fit:
- Small teams
- Early-stage environments
- Emergency response situations

In these cases, ITIL principles still apply, but with lighter controls.

## Final reminder

ITIL provides structure.  
AI provides leverage.

Neither replaces leadership, judgment, or responsibility.

Use both to deliver value, not to hide behind process.
